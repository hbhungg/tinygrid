TinyGrid

Description

Tinygrid is a project to develop an optimal battery schedule and an optimal lecture schedule in Monash University Clayton Campus, based on predictions of future values of energy demand and production.

Description

One of the most important measures to deal with climate change is through the use of renewable energy such as wind and solar. However, renewable energy cannot be produced on command, which means energy storage is required. At the same time, storing energy is very expensive and may cause a loss of energy stored. Therefore, we need to forecast the energy demand and production from renewables, in order to optimally schedule energy storage such as batteries. This enables an optimal charging and discharging schedule, based on the required demand.
This is where our projecy TinyGrid steps in. TinyGrid at its core has a prediction module that predicts solar energy production and building energy usage from the Monash Microgrid system, and an optimization module which schedules building activities and batteries to minimise energy wastage. Presented as a website interface that lets users interact with figures and values about the evaluation of forecasting and optimization solutions. Thus, TinyGrid solves the problem of optimising renewable energy storage and usage.

Data

Forecasting

Energy demand for all 6 buildings are forecasted. The forecasted energy demand are done in a time series data set where a 15 minute time step is used. Several models are trained in order to forecast the energy demand from 1st of October to 30th of November 2020.
The models used are lasso and random forest. Out of all these models, we found out that the best model for this dataset is lasso.
Solar production for all 6 solar panels are forecasted. The forecasted solar production are done in a time series data set where a 15 minute time step is used. Several models are trained in order to forecast the power production from 1st of October to 30th of
November 2020. The mean MASE calculated of both the energy production and solar production for our model is 0.7759 which puts our team in 5th position of the IEEE-CIS competition.
